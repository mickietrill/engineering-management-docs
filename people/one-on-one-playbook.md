# One-on-One Playbook

This playbook defines best practices, principles, and practical frameworks for running effective one-on-one meetings in engineering teams.
Its goal is to help managers build trust, support growth, surface issues early, and continuously improve performance and engagement through structured yet flexible one-on-one conversations.

Well-run one-on-ones are one of the most powerful tools for:

- Employee development and retention
- Early problem detection
- Performance improvement
- Building strong manager–employee relationships
- Creating psychological safety

This document provides:

- Clear principles and structures for one-on-ones
- Practical question frameworks
- Guidance for difficult conversations
- Best practices for tracking progress
- Standardized templates for documentation

## 0. Purpose and Scope

### Goals of one-on-one meetings

One-on-one meetings are a core leadership tool used to:

- Build trust and psychological safety between manager and team member
- Understand individual motivation, concerns, and career goals
- Identify blockers and risks early before they escalate
- Provide continuous feedback and coaching
- Align individual goals with team and business objectives
- Track growth, performance, and well-being over time

Effective one-on-ones are not about status updates — they are about people, clarity, and long-term effectiveness.

When run consistently, they become the primary channel for:

- Engagement
- Retention
- Performance improvement
- Leadership development

### Who should run them

One-on-ones should be run by:

- Engineering Managers
- Tech Leads (when responsible for people growth)
- Team Leads
- CTO / Head of Engineering (with direct reports)

They are mandatory for:

- All direct reports
- New hires during onboarding period
- Team members in growth or performance improvement phases

Optional (but recommended) for:

- Senior engineers and key contributors
- Cross-functional leads when strong collaboration is required

The responsibility for scheduling, preparing, and following up always lies with the manager.

### What one-on-ones are (and are not)

#### One-on-ones ARE:

- A dedicated space for the employee’s topics and concerns
- A coaching and feedback channel
- A place to discuss growth, motivation, and challenges
- A safe environment for honest communication
- A tool for early problem detection

#### One-on-ones are NOT:

- Status meetings or task reviews
- Sprint planning or backlog grooming sessions
- Performance evaluation meetings only
- Ad-hoc problem-solving sessions for daily work
- A replacement for team meetings

Operational topics may be discussed, but they should never dominate the conversation.

The primary focus must always remain on:

- The person
- Their effectiveness
- Their experience in the team and organization

## 1. Core Principles of Effective One-on-Ones

Effective one-on-one meetings are built on a small set of core principles. Without them, one-on-ones quickly turn into status updates, formal check-ins, or wasted time.

### Calm and focused environment

One-on-one meetings should be held in a private, distraction-free setting that supports open and relaxed communication.

Guidelines:

- Avoid interruptions and multitasking
- Choose a quiet and comfortable environment
- Allow enough time without rushing
- Ensure confidentiality

A calm environment reinforces psychological safety and improves the quality of conversations.

### Trust and psychological safety

Trust is the foundation of every successful one-on-one.

Employees must feel safe to:

- Speak openly about problems and concerns
- Share mistakes and uncertainties
- Give honest feedback to their manager
- Discuss personal challenges that impact work

Manager responsibilities:

- Keep conversations confidential (unless explicitly agreed otherwise)
- React calmly to negative feedback or bad news
- Avoid judgment and defensiveness
- Follow through on commitments

Without psychological safety, one-on-ones become superficial and lose their value.

### Employee-driven agenda

The primary owner of the one-on-one agenda is the employee.

Guidelines:

- Start each meeting by asking what the employee wants to discuss
- Prioritize their topics before manager topics
- Encourage preparation in advance (notes, questions, concerns)

Typical flow:

1. Employee topics
2. Manager topics (feedback, updates, coaching)
3. Action items and next steps

This ensures one-on-ones focus on what matters most to the team member.

### Consistency and regularity

One-on-ones must happen consistently.

Recommended cadence:

- Weekly or bi-weekly for most team members
- More frequent for new hires or performance improvement periods
- Less frequent (monthly) for very senior contributors if mutually agreed

Rules:

- Do not cancel unless absolutely necessary
- If canceled, reschedule as soon as possible
- Treat one-on-ones as a top priority meeting

Inconsistent one-on-ones quickly erode trust and engagement.

### Listening over talking

The manager’s primary role in one-on-ones is to listen.

Guidelines:

- Let the employee speak most of the time (ideally 60–70%)
- Ask open-ended questions
- Avoid interrupting
- Clarify and reflect back what you hear

Good one-on-ones are driven by curiosity, not lectures.

Common mistake:

- Turning the meeting into a manager monologue or advice session

### Action and follow-up

Every effective one-on-one should produce clear outcomes when needed.

This may include:

- Action items
- Decisions
- Follow-up topics
- Experiments or changes

Best practices:

- Write down agreed actions
- Assign clear ownership
- Review previous actions in the next meeting

Without follow-up, one-on-ones lose credibility and become just conversations with no impact.

## 2. Frequency and Format

The effectiveness of one-on-one meetings depends heavily on their regularity and structure.
Clear expectations around cadence, duration, and format help ensure consistency and long-term value.

### Recommended cadence

The ideal frequency depends on seniority, role, and current context.

General recommendations:

- **Weekly**  
  Best for:
  - New hires  
  - Junior and mid-level engineers  
  - Team members needing close coaching or support  
  - Periods of high change or stress  

- **Bi-weekly**  
  Best for:
  - Stable senior engineers  
  - Team members with high autonomy  

- **Monthly (rare, exceptional cases)**  
  Only for:
  - Very senior contributors  
  - When trust and communication are already strong  

Guidelines:

- Default to weekly or bi-weekly
- Increase frequency during onboarding or performance challenges
- Do not reduce frequency purely to “save time”

Consistency matters more than finding the perfect cadence.

### Meeting duration

Recommended durations:

- **30 minutes** — minimum effective length  
- **45 minutes** — ideal for most situations  
- **60 minutes** — useful for deeper coaching or complex discussions  

Guidelines:

- Shorter than 30 minutes often becomes rushed
- Longer than 60 minutes usually leads to fatigue
- Keep duration consistent for predictability

Choose a duration that allows meaningful conversation without pressure.

### Remote vs in-person

Both remote and in-person one-on-ones can be effective if handled well.

#### Remote one-on-ones

Best practices:

- Always use video when possible
- Choose a quiet environment
- Avoid multitasking
- Maintain eye contact through the camera

Advantages:

- Easy scheduling
- Comfortable for distributed teams
- Often more focused

#### In-person one-on-ones

Best practices:

- Choose a private and comfortable location
- Consider walking meetings for informal discussions
- Avoid open spaces where others can overhear

Advantages:

- Stronger personal connection
- Easier to read body language
- Often better for sensitive conversations

Use whichever format fits your team setup — consistency is more important than format.

### When to reschedule or skip

One-on-ones should rarely be skipped.

Reschedule when:

- Critical production incidents
- Emergencies
- Unavoidable conflicts

Rules:

- Always reschedule canceled one-on-ones
- Do not simply skip and wait for the next cycle
- Communicate early if changes are needed

Avoid skipping for:

- “Busy week”
- Regular meetings
- Minor conflicts

Skipping regularly signals that one-on-ones are not a priority — and damages trust.

## 3. Preparing for a One-on-One

Effective one-on-ones start before the meeting itself.
Proper preparation by both the manager and the employee ensures focused, meaningful, and productive conversations.

Lack of preparation often leads to:

- Surface-level discussions
- Repeating the same topics
- Missed follow-ups
- Reduced trust in the process

Preparation should be lightweight but intentional.

### Manager preparation

Before each one-on-one, the manager should:

- Review notes from the previous meeting
- Check agreed action items and follow-ups
- Reflect on recent performance, behavior, and interactions
- Prepare topics related to:
  - Growth and development
  - Feedback (positive and constructive)
  - Team dynamics
  - Workload and priorities

Helpful questions for managers:

- What progress has been made since the last one-on-one?
- Are there any concerns I should raise?
- Are there achievements I should recognize?
- Is the employee showing signs of stress or disengagement?

Guidelines:

- Avoid coming in with a rigid script
- Have a few focus points, not a full agenda
- Leave space for employee-driven topics

### Employee preparation

Employees should be encouraged to prepare their own agenda.

They should think about:

- Topics they want to discuss
- Challenges or blockers
- Feedback for the manager or team
- Career development questions
- Workload or prioritization concerns

Helpful prompts for employees:

- What is going well?
- What is frustrating or blocking me?
- What support do I need?
- What do I want to improve or learn?
- Is there anything I’ve been hesitant to bring up?

Guidelines:

- The meeting should primarily serve the employee
- Encourage honest and open sharing
- Preparation can be as simple as a short bullet list

### Reviewing previous action items

At the beginning of each one-on-one:

- Review action items from the previous meeting
- Check progress and outcomes
- Discuss obstacles if something was not completed

Best practices:

- Keep action items visible in shared notes or a tracking document
- Be realistic with commitments
- Close items once completed

This creates:

- Accountability
- Continuity between meetings
- Real progress over time

### Using data and feedback

When appropriate, support discussions with objective data and feedback.

Examples:

- Performance metrics
- Delivery timelines
- Quality indicators
- Peer feedback
- Customer or stakeholder input

Guidelines:

- Use data to inform, not to attack
- Combine metrics with context
- Focus on patterns, not isolated incidents

Good data usage helps:

- Ground conversations in reality
- Reduce emotional tension
- Make feedback more constructive

Avoid:

- Overloading the meeting with metrics
- Using data as a weapon
- Ignoring qualitative feedback

Preparation should enable deeper, more focused conversations — not turn one-on-ones into status meetings.

## 4. Typical One-on-One Structure

While one-on-ones should remain flexible and employee-driven, having a consistent high-level structure helps keep meetings focused, productive, and balanced.

A typical one-on-one can be divided into five main parts:

1. Check-in  
2. Employee topics  
3. Manager topics  
4. Feedback exchange  
5. Action items and wrap-up  

This structure ensures both sides are heard and that conversations lead to real outcomes.

### Check-in

Start the meeting with a short personal and work-related check-in.

Purpose:

- Build rapport and trust
- Set the emotional tone of the meeting
- Surface any immediate concerns

Example questions:

- How are you feeling this week?
- How’s work been going lately?
- Anything on your mind before we dive in?

Guidelines:

- Keep it brief (5 minutes)
- Listen actively
- Don’t rush into tasks immediately

This is not a status update — it’s about the person.

### Employee topics

This should be the main portion of the meeting.

The employee leads the agenda here.

Common topics include:

- Current challenges or blockers
- Workload and priorities
- Team dynamics
- Career growth and learning
- Feedback for the manager
- Concerns or ideas

Guidelines:

- Let the employee speak first
- Avoid interrupting or immediately solving everything
- Ask open-ended questions
- Clarify and explore deeper when needed

Good manager prompts:

- What would you like to focus on today?
- What’s been most challenging recently?
- Where can I support you better?

### Manager topics

After employee topics are covered, the manager brings their own points.

Typical areas:

- Feedback on performance or behavior
- Business updates that affect the employee
- Expectations and priorities
- Growth opportunities

Guidelines:

- Keep it focused and relevant
- Avoid turning it into a status meeting
- Be clear and direct
- Balance positive and constructive feedback

This section should complement — not dominate — the meeting.

### Feedback exchange

Create space for two-way feedback.

This may overlap with previous sections but should be intentional.

Types of feedback:

- Positive recognition
- Developmental feedback
- Process or collaboration improvements
- Manager effectiveness feedback

Guidelines:

- Be specific and factual
- Focus on behaviors, not personalities
- Encourage employee feedback for the manager
- Thank people for honest input

Example prompts:

- What’s something I could do better as your manager?
- Is there anything that’s been frustrating lately?
- What’s working well in our collaboration?

Best practice: Start feedback with positive observations before addressing improvement areas.

This helps:

- Reinforce effective behaviors
- Reduce defensiveness
- Build motivation and trust

### Action items and wrap-up

End each one-on-one with clear next steps.

Should include:

- Agreed actions for the employee
- Agreed actions for the manager
- Follow-ups for future meetings

Best practices:

- Write down action items
- Assign clear ownership
- Set realistic timelines
- Review them at the next one-on-one

Example:

```text
- Manager to clarify project priorities by Friday
- Employee to explore training options for skill X
- Follow up on workload balance next meeting
```

Finish with a quick closing:

- Anything else we should cover?
- Does this plan sound good?

This reinforces accountability and momentum.

This structure is a guideline, not a rigid agenda.

Great one-on-ones adapt to the situation while keeping the same core flow:
connection → employee focus → alignment → feedback → action.

## 5. Question Frameworks

Well-structured questions help turn one-on-ones into meaningful conversations rather than status updates.

This section provides practical question frameworks grouped by common one-on-one focus areas.
Managers should not ask all questions in one meeting — use them selectively based on context and priorities.

The goal is to:

- Encourage open discussion
- Identify issues early
- Support growth and engagement
- Strengthen trust

### Career and Growth

Focus: long-term development, skills, aspirations, and career direction.

Example questions:

- What skills would you like to develop next?
- Where do you see yourself in 1–2 years?
- What kind of projects excite you most?
- What work helps you learn the most?
- Is there something new you’d like to try or own?

Deeper follow-ups:

- What’s blocking your growth right now?
- How can I support your development better?
- What resources or opportunities would help?

Use these regularly (not every meeting) to keep growth visible.

### Motivation and Engagement

Focus: understanding what energizes or drains the employee.

Example questions:

- What’s been most enjoyable at work lately?
- What’s been frustrating or demotivating?
- Which tasks give you energy?
- Which tasks drain you the most?
- Do you feel your work is impactful?

Deeper follow-ups:

- What would make your work more meaningful?
- Are we using your strengths enough?
- What would you change if you could?

These questions help prevent burnout and disengagement.

### Feedback and Blockers

Focus: improving performance, removing obstacles, and improving collaboration.

Example questions:

- What’s slowing you down right now?
- Where are you getting stuck?
- Is there anything unclear about priorities or expectations?
- What support do you need from me?
- Is there a process that isn’t working well?

Feedback-oriented prompts:

- What feedback do you have for me?
- Is there something I should do differently?
- What’s one thing we could improve as a team?

Use these to surface problems early instead of during performance reviews.

### Team Dynamics

Focus: collaboration, communication, and team health.

Example questions:

- How do you feel about the team right now?
- Is collaboration working well?
- Any tensions or conflicts I should be aware of?
- Who do you enjoy working with most and why?
- Where do you think the team could improve?

Deeper follow-ups:

- Do you feel heard in the team?
- Are decisions clear and transparent?
- Is workload distributed fairly?

These help maintain a healthy team environment.

### Well-being

Focus: personal sustainability, stress levels, and work-life balance.

Example questions:

- How’s your workload feeling lately?
- Are you feeling stressed or overwhelmed?
- Are you able to disconnect after work?
- Is anything outside of work affecting you right now?
- Do you feel you have enough time to focus properly?

Guidelines:

- Keep this supportive, not intrusive
- Respect personal boundaries
- Offer help, flexibility, or resources when needed

These conversations build trust and prevent burnout.

Best practices for using question frameworks:

- Rotate topics across meetings
- Adapt questions to the individual
- Listen more than you speak
- Follow up on previous discussions
- Turn insights into actions

Questions are tools — not a checklist.

The quality of listening and follow-up matters more than the number of questions asked.

## 6. Handling Difficult Topics

One-on-ones often surface sensitive or challenging topics. Handling them well builds trust, while handling them poorly damages relationships and morale.

The manager’s role is to:

- Create a safe environment for honest discussion
- Listen without judgment
- Stay calm and objective
- Focus on solutions and support

This section covers common difficult areas and how to approach them effectively.

### Performance Issues

When performance concerns arise, the goal is improvement — not blame.

Guidelines:

- Be specific and fact-based
- Focus on behaviors and outcomes, not personality
- Explain impact clearly
- Collaborate on improvement plans

Example approach:

- Describe the issue clearly  
  (“I noticed the last two deadlines were missed.”)

- Explain impact  
  (“This delayed the release and affected other teams.”)

- Ask for perspective  
  (“What happened from your side?”)

- Agree on next steps  
  (“What can we change to avoid this going forward?”)

Avoid:

- Vague feedback (“You need to do better”)
- Public criticism
- Emotional reactions

### Conflicts

Conflicts may exist between team members, teams, or with stakeholders.

Guidelines:

- Listen fully to the employee’s perspective
- Avoid taking sides immediately
- Focus on facts and behaviors
- Encourage respectful communication

Helpful questions:

- What exactly happened?
- How did it impact your work?
- What outcome would you like to see?
- Have you tried addressing it directly?

Manager actions may include:

- Facilitating a joint conversation
- Clarifying expectations
- Escalating when necessary

Avoid ignoring conflicts — they rarely resolve themselves.

### Burnout Signals

Burnout often appears gradually and can be hard for employees to express directly.

Common signals:

- Decreased energy or engagement
- Increased mistakes or missed deadlines
- Irritability or withdrawal
- Working excessive hours

Guidelines:

- Address concerns early
- Show empathy and support
- Avoid blaming

Example questions:

- You seem more stressed lately — how are you feeling?
- Is your workload manageable right now?
- What’s been most exhausting recently?

Possible actions:

- Adjust workload or priorities
- Encourage time off
- Provide additional support or resources

Burnout is a system problem, not a personal failure.

### Compensation and Promotions

These topics are sensitive and emotionally charged.

Guidelines:

- Be transparent within company policy limits
- Set clear expectations
- Avoid vague promises
- Focus on criteria and growth paths

Helpful approaches:

- Explain how compensation reviews work
- Clarify promotion requirements
- Provide concrete feedback on readiness
- Create development plans tied to growth goals

Avoid:

- Making guarantees you can’t keep
- Comparing employees to others
- Dodging the conversation

If you don’t have answers, commit to following up.

### Personal Challenges

Employees may share personal issues affecting work.

Guidelines:

- Listen with empathy
- Respect privacy
- Don’t push for details
- Focus on how you can support

Example responses:

- Thank you for sharing
- How can I help or support you right now?
- Would flexibility help?

Possible support options:

- Temporary workload adjustments
- Flexible hours
- Time off
- Directing to company support resources

Avoid acting as a therapist — your role is to support professionally.

Best practices for difficult topics:

- Stay calm and composed
- Listen more than you talk
- Validate feelings without necessarily agreeing
- Focus on next steps and solutions
- Follow up in future one-on-ones

Handled well, difficult conversations strengthen trust and performance.
Handled poorly, they create fear and disengagement.

## 7. Tracking Progress and Accountability

One-on-ones are only effective when conversations lead to actions and improvements.

Without proper tracking and follow-up:

- Issues repeat
- Commitments are forgotten
- Growth stalls
- Trust decreases

This section outlines how to track outcomes and ensure accountability without turning one-on-ones into micromanagement sessions.

### Action Items

Every meaningful one-on-one should result in clear action items when appropriate.

Action items should be:

- Specific
- Measurable when possible
- Owned by someone (manager or employee)
- Time-bound or reviewed regularly

Examples:

- Employee: Prepare a proposal for improving deployment process by next week
- Manager: Get clarification on promotion criteria from HR by next meeting
- Employee: Shadow senior engineer on upcoming project

Best practices:

- Write action items down immediately
- Confirm agreement on each action item
- Avoid vague actions like “think about it”

---

### Follow-ups

Follow-up is what builds trust and shows commitment.

Guidelines:

- Review previous action items at the start of each one-on-one
- Ask about progress and blockers
- Adjust timelines if needed
- Close completed actions explicitly

Example flow:

```text
Last time we agreed you would look into X — how is that going?
Is there anything blocking you?
Do we need to adjust the plan?
```

Avoid:

- Forgetting previous commitments
- Treating action items casually
- Moving on without closure

### Patterns and Trends

One-on-ones provide valuable long-term insights when tracked consistently.

Look for patterns such as:

- Repeated blockers
- Declining motivation
- Growth stagnation
- Frequent workload stress
- Communication issues

Manager responsibilities:

- Notice recurring themes
- Connect individual feedback to systemic problems
- Raise organizational issues when necessary
- Adjust team processes accordingly

Examples:

- Multiple team members struggling with unclear priorities
- Repeated burnout signals across the team
- Common complaints about a specific process

These patterns often indicate larger organizational problems.

### Documentation Best Practices

One-on-one documentation should be:

- Simple and lightweight
- Consistent across meetings
- Secure and private
- Focused on key points and actions

Recommended to document:

- Date and participants
- Key discussion topics
- Action items with owners
- Follow-up notes
- Important concerns or feedback

Avoid:

- Writing full transcripts
- Over-documenting personal details
- Using documentation as performance surveillance

Privacy considerations:

- Keep notes confidential
- Share only what’s necessary
- Follow company data policies

Well-maintained notes enable:

- Better follow-ups
- Clear growth tracking
- Historical context
- More effective management

Strong tracking turns one-on-ones into a continuous improvement loop rather than isolated conversations.

## 8. Common Anti-Patterns

The following anti-patterns significantly reduce the effectiveness of one-on-one meetings. They often lead to disengagement, lack of trust, and missed issues.

Avoid them deliberately.

### Turning one-on-ones into status meetings

Using one-on-ones primarily to:

- Review tasks
- Check progress on tickets
- Discuss sprint updates

Impact:

- Employee stops bringing real concerns
- Meetings become redundant with team syncs
- Trust and openness decrease

Better approach:

- Use team meetings, standups, and project tools for status
- Keep one-on-ones focused on people, growth, and challenges

### Canceling regularly

Frequently postponing or canceling one-on-ones due to:

- Busy schedules
- “More important” meetings
- Short-term priorities

Impact:

- Sends signal that people are not a priority
- Breaks consistency and trust
- Reduces engagement

Guidelines:

- Treat one-on-ones as high-priority commitments
- Cancel only when absolutely necessary
- Reschedule as soon as possible

### Talking too much as a manager

Managers dominating the conversation by:

- Giving long monologues
- Turning meetings into lectures
- Constantly offering solutions

Impact:

- Employee feels unheard
- Valuable insights are missed
- Relationship becomes one-directional

Better approach:

- Ask open-ended questions
- Listen actively
- Let silence create space for reflection

Rule of thumb:

> The employee should speak at least 60–70% of the time.

### No follow-ups

Discussing issues and ideas but never:

- Acting on them
- Revisiting them
- Closing action items

Impact:

- Employee loses trust in the process
- Problems persist
- One-on-ones feel pointless

Better approach:

- Track action items
- Review them consistently
- Show progress and accountability

### Lack of trust

When employees:

- Don’t feel safe sharing concerns
- Avoid difficult topics
- Provide only surface-level answers

Common causes:

- Past negative reactions
- Breach of confidentiality
- Using one-on-ones for performance punishment

Impact:

- Real issues stay hidden
- Engagement decreases
- Problems escalate silently

Better approach:

- Be consistent and fair
- Respect confidentiality
- React constructively to feedback
- Encourage honesty without fear

Avoiding these anti-patterns is as important as following best practices.

## 9. Exceptions and Adaptation

While the principles and structure described in this playbook should be the default approach for one-on-one meetings, they are not meant to be applied rigidly in every situation. Different roles, experience levels, and contexts may require adjustments. The goal is effectiveness — not strict adherence to a fixed format.

### Senior engineers

For highly experienced engineers or technical leaders:

Typical adaptations:

- Less focus on day-to-day support
- More focus on:
  - Strategic topics
  - System design decisions
  - Cross-team collaboration
  - Career trajectory and influence
- More peer-like discussion style

Guidelines:

- Treat one-on-ones as strategic conversations
- Use them to gather insights about the organization and technical health
- Encourage ownership and initiative

Avoid:

- Micromanagement
- Over-structuring the conversation

### New hires

For new team members (especially in the first 1–3 months):

Typical adaptations:

- More frequent one-on-ones (weekly or even twice per week initially)
- More structured agenda
- More focus on onboarding progress

Topics to emphasize:

- Understanding expectations
- Access to tools and systems
- Team dynamics
- Early feedback
- Blockers and confusion

Guidelines:

- Proactively ask detailed questions
- Help navigate the organization
- Ensure fast feedback loops

Goal:

> Accelerate onboarding and build trust early.

### Performance improvement periods

When an employee is in a performance improvement or support phase:

Typical adaptations:

- Increased frequency (weekly minimum)
- More concrete goals and action items
- Clear tracking of progress

Focus areas:

- Specific expectations
- Measurable improvements
- Support needed
- Feedback on recent performance

Guidelines:

- Be clear and direct
- Avoid vague feedback
- Document action items carefully
- Maintain supportive tone

Avoid:

- Turning one-on-ones into punishment sessions
- Ignoring emotional impact

### Cultural considerations

Cultural backgrounds can strongly influence communication styles.

Common differences include:

- Comfort with direct feedback
- Willingness to speak openly to managers
- Attitudes toward authority
- Preference for structured vs informal conversations

Guidelines:

- Adapt communication style to the individual
- Encourage openness gradually if needed
- Be explicit about expectations and psychological safety
- Avoid assumptions based on culture alone

Goal:

> Create an environment where each individual feels safe and understood.

Flexibility within a clear framework is what makes one-on-ones truly effective.

Use this playbook as a guide — not as a rigid script.
